Euroleague: The Evolution of Pace Over Time

An Annual Review

Ελληνικά English

Historical Analysis

In recent years, it is mentioned more and more frequently that European basketball has become faster. Faster possessions, more immediate inbound passes after a basket, more fast breaks, while regulations have also been added to help increase the speed of the game (see ACB and the rule for inbounding the ball when it goes out of bounds without referee intervention).

At first glance, it seems correct. But does it hold true generally? Has the game actually become faster? If so, then the points scored by teams should be more, right? Or do faster possessions lead to more rushed decisions? Are high-pace teams the ones that perform better throughout the season? All these questions will be properly answered in this project.

Before we begin, I should mention that for this specific project, data tables were used containing a) advanced stats for every team that has competed in the Euroleague (from the 2000-2001 season until today) and b) basic stats for every player who has played in the Euroleague (from the 2000-2001 season until today). What are the metrics that show team possessions and their efficiency in them? We have Pace, which is a team's possessions per game (40 minutes), Offensive Rating, which shows a team's average points scored per 100 possessions, and True Shooting %, which measures a team's scoring efficiency across free throws, 2-pointers, and 3-pointers.

Beyond a potential increase in possessions, it is crucial to examine whether this increase improves team performance, raising the points they score, or if teams achieve better efficiency in scoring (True Shooting Percentage). Obviously, increasing possessions and making the game faster makes no sense if team efficiency decreases.

Pace vs Offensive Rating & True Shooting %

Average Pace vs Average Off. Rating

In the first years of the Euroleague, average possessions per game were above 73 (73.9) and the average off. rating was around 108. Over a 20-year span, and following several fluctuations, while average possessions have decreased by about 1.5 (72.4), the off. rating has increased to 117.88 on average (about 10 more compared to before). Thus, even though possessions have decreased (there is an upward trend in recent years), teams score more points per possession (1.627 versus 1.46), highlighting the difference in efficiency.

Average Pace vs Average True Shooting %

The same can be said for the relationship between avg pace and true shooting percentage. TS% has increased from 56% to 59%. A 3% difference might not seem significant, but in a competition where an average of 20 teams participate each year, increasing scoring efficiency (TS%) by 3% with 1.5 fewer average possessions is a clear sign of the tournament's evolution.

Until 2014, during the downward trajectory of the teams' avg pace, TS% would either remain the same or decrease. This means that the teams' scoring efficiency was declining. From 2018 until today (with the exception of 2022), alongside the increase in possessions from around 69.2 to 72.5, there is an increase in team scoring efficiency of about 1%. It seems like a negligible amount, but combined with the increase in possessions, even remaining at the same values would be a positive scenario. TS% began to increase before the rise of avg pace, from 2016 to 2018, by over 2.5%.

It is worth mentioning that since 2022, with the increase in average possessions per game (from 68.1 to 72.4), TS% also increased from 56.9% to 58.9%.


Does Higher Pace Imply a Higher Offensive Rating?

Most people would believe that as possessions increase for each team, the points they score will also increase. Based on the charts, however, clear examples are observed that refute this claim.

  • During the 2016-2018 period, pace decreased from 70.9 to 69.2, but offensive rating increased from 106.6 to 115.9
  • During the 2001-2004 period, pace decreased from around 74 to 69.6, but offensive rating increased from 108.1 to 111.8
  • During the 2004-2006 period, pace remained almost identical from 69.6 to 69.9, but offensive rating decreased from 111.8 to 105.8

History has shown, therefore, that increasing the speed of the game does not imply an increase in the teams' scoring output.


Does Higher Offensive Rating Imply a Higher Pace?

Above we proved that a team having a higher pace (more possessions) does not imply that the points scored per 100 possessions (offensive rating) will be more. But what happens when the inverse occurs? That is, when a team's offensive rating increases, will its pace increase as well?

Just like before, we will answer by taking into account the line charts of pace and offensive rating. Looking only at these, we identify characteristic examples that refute this claim.

  • During the 2001-2004 period, offensive rating increased from 108.1 to 111.8, but pace decreased from 73.95 to 69.67
  • During the 2017-2018 period, offensive rating increased from 110.6 to 115.9, but pace decreased from 71.01 to 69.2

While the examples proving the opposite are not numerous, they are sufficient to reach a safe conclusion that an increase in offensive rating does not imply an increase in the pace of Euroleague teams.

Euroleague Breakdown Into Periods

Since basketball was created as a sport, several changes have occurred that altered the way teams play. From the addition of the 24-second shot clock (1954 in the NBA, 1956 in FIBA) and the 3-point line (1979 in the NBA, 1984 in FIBA), to more recent changes like the coach's challenge and the use of instant replay to contest referee decisions, the game has passed through many phases while remaining one of the most popular sports worldwide.

Since the emergence of the Euroleague as a competition, several rule changes have been made, consequently affecting the teams' style of play. We will examine if during these periods when changes were implemented, the volume of team possessions and their scoring efficiency were affected.

2000 - 2008

Initial Period of the Euroleague.

The competition begins with an average pace close to 74 possessions per game, a fairly high average considering we are talking about the Euroleague. By the end of the period, the game becomes slower (the pace drops to 70 possessions on average per team), while both offensive rating and true shooting % show the same trajectory for each year, ending up at values slightly lower than the initial ones.

2008 - 2016

Changes: In the 08-09 season, playoffs changed to a best-of-five series. In the 10-11 season, the 3-point line changed from 6.25 meters to 6.75 meters. In the 14-15 season, the shot clock reset after an offensive rebound changed from 24 to 14 seconds.

Here, greater monotony appears in the teams' average pace. It starts at 70 possessions per match and concludes at approximately 71 per team. On the other hand, both offensive rating and true shooting % will experience several fluctuations during this period and will finish at values slightly below the starting ones (Offensive rating from 108 points down to 106.6 and TS% from 56.5% down to 55.3%).

2016 - 2023

Changes: In the 16-17 season groups were abolished; everyone plays everyone, home and away (round-robin style).

During this period, pace changes move roughly similarly to the previous two. The only difference is that at the end of the period, a slight decrease in average possessions occurs (around 0.5-1.0). The off. rating will rise above 110 points from 2017 onwards (round-robin games) and will not decrease at all until the end of the period, where it concludes at 115 points. The same applies to scoring consistency, as TS% does not drop below 56.9% and finishes at 58.5% at the end of the period.

2023 - Today

Changes: The play-in tournament started in 2023. The league teams expanded to 20 in 2024.

The shortest period compared to the others and the one we are currently going through. There is a continuous annual increase in pace from 69.5 possessions per game to approximately 72.5. Here, the off. rating has increased up to 117.8 points and TS% remains steadily above 58%, reaching close to 59% now in 2026.

Pace & Offensive Rating By Team Type

In the Euroleague, there are different types of teams with different styles of play. The most prevalent ones that we will focus on the most are teams with at least one dominant scorer or one dominant creator (heliocentric) and teams with heavy ball movement in their offenses (motion offense).

Euroleague teams will be segmented according to these traits, and for each category, we will test if a trait impacts the volume of possessions or the number of points per 100 possessions.

But how can we distinguish from statistics alone whether a team features a dominant scorer/creator or high ball movement? We will be aided by each player's individual USG% and individual AST%, as well as the team's overall metrics.

USG% is an advanced statistic that estimates the percentage of team plays completed by a player while they are on the court. An offensive possession is completed by a player when they attempt a shot, a free throw, or turn the ball over at the end of it. A player with a USG% of at least 28% is considered dominant in finishing plays for their team.

AST% is another advanced statistic that estimates the percentage of field goals that come from an assist by a specific player while on the floor. When referring to the team, however, it displays the percentage of the team's total field goals that come from assists in general. A player with an AST% greater than 35% is considered a dominant creator, since over a third of their team's baskets result from their assist. On the other hand, a team with an AST% of at least 63% is considered a team with high ball circulation in its offense.

In our evaluation, we also calculate the Offensive Rating to Pace Ratio, which shows the average points per possession that teams achieve.

The effectiveness of these teams is not measured solely by offensive rating, but also by True Shooting Percentage (TS%). All 24 champions recorded from 2002 onwards had a TS% above the league average at the time. For Final Four participants, this figure stands at 94.57% (87/92 teams).

Conclusions

The analysis above allows us to draw several conclusions regarding the nature of modern European basketball.

Regarding the first question, whether an increase in possessions implies an increase in team scoring, we can say that there is no full correlation. The competition went through periods where pace decreased gradually, yet offensive rating surged, debunking the myth that more possessions lead to more points. Instead, the quality of execution and tactical plan on offense outweigh simple speed.

In contrast, there is a major qualitative upgrade. Comparing the fastest teams of the early and late years of the competition, and while older teams had more possessions, the recent ones are considerably more effective on offense (11 points higher in offensive rating and a 2.5% increased true shooting percentage), indicating that modern basketball has become more efficient.

It is quite interesting that 2018 marks a turning point for the competition. The average team points per 100 possessions, along with true shooting %, skyrocketed to higher levels and have followed an upward, if not stable, path ever since. 2018 coincides chronologically with the change in the competition's format and the decision for all teams to play against each other. Also, a few years prior, the distance of the 3-point line was altered, which might have initially influenced teams to show greater efficiency on offense. Naturally, these claims cannot be validated by statistics alone, but it is something worth mentioning.

Furthermore, 2018 also signals the beginning of combining fast/efficient play with ball movement on offense. While previously teams with high ball movement mostly failed to surpass the average pace and offensive rating, from 2018 onwards, these teams as a whole record values above the average, transforming ball circulation into a catalyst for an efficient rhythm.

The best teams each year feature high offensive rating and true shooting % as their main characteristics. The possessions they have in each game are not necessarily above the average, showing us that they can dictate their rhythm and achieve greater effectiveness without relying on numerous possessions.

Therefore, has the game become faster? The answer is no, but it has become higher in quality and efficiency. The average number of possessions nowadays has indeed increased compared to previous years, but it has not reached the levels of the competition's initial seasons. Many believe the game has become faster because they see higher scores at the end of each match. It was proven, however, that higher scores are not the result of more possessions, but of the rise in team efficiency (offensive rating and true shooting %). Modern European basketball has become faster in execution and decision-making. Teams now exploit ball movement and better shot selection to produce more points by the end of the game.
